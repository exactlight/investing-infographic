The Creative Investor's Toolkit: A Strategy Breakdown

Subject-To (Sub2)

Acquiring property by taking over the seller's existing mortgage payments without formally assuming the loan. The deed transfers, but the loan stays in the seller's name.

This visualization highlights the core components of a Subject-To deal. While offering low entry costs and access to favorable loan terms, the primary risk for the investor and seller is the lender's right to invoke the "Due-on-Sale" clause.

Seller Financing

The property owner acts as the bank, financing the purchase directly for the buyer. Terms like interest rate and down payment are fully negotiable.

Seller financing offers tremendous flexibility. This chart shows the distribution of benefits, with sellers gaining a wider buyer pool and passive income, while buyers get easier qualification and custom terms. It is most ideal when the seller owns the property free and clear.

BRRRR Method

Buy, Rehab, Rent, Refinance, Repeat. A strategy to build a rental portfolio by recycling capital through forced appreciation and cash-out refinances.

The BRRRR method is a powerful engine for portfolio growth. Its success hinges on the 'Refinance' stage, where the ability to pull capital out is dependent on a successful appraisal (ARV) and favorable lending conditions.

Anatomy of a "Terms" Deal: The 3 Paydays System

Pioneered by Chris Prefontaine, this system layers a lease purchase on top of a Sub2 or Seller Finance acquisition, creating multiple profit centers from a single transaction over time.

1

Payday #1: Upfront & Non-Refundable

The investor collects a significant, non-refundable down payment from a qualified tenant-buyer. This immediately de-risks the deal and provides initial profit, often covering any cash paid to the original seller.

2

Payday #2: Monthly Cash Flow

For the duration of the lease term (typically 1-4 years), the investor earns the "spread" between the rent collected from the tenant-buyer and the mortgage payment made to the original seller's lender (in a Sub2) or directly to the seller (in owner financing).

3

Payday #3: Backend Profit

When the tenant-buyer exercises their option and purchases the property, the investor realizes the final profit. This includes the appreciation markup (difference between the investor's purchase price and the tenant-buyer's price) plus all the principal paydown on the underlying loan that occurred during the lease term.
